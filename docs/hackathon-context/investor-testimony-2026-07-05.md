## Whose testimony this is

- An investor who invests in code-review-related startups (has invested in multiple companies, and mentioned having invested in Openhome, among others).
- Personally works on the front line of PR review in their day job as well.

## Extracted testimony (2 points)

### 1. Backing for the Problem — "PRs don't scale"

- In their day job, this person carries the real-world pain that "**PRs themselves don't scale**. Some of them are impossible to read. The changes are too extensive to review."
- A firsthand voice from someone on the front line independently corroborating the Problem GHeart claims (PR flood, unreviewable volume, bottlenecking).

Transcript (verbatim, relevant excerpt):
> "in my day job it was basically PR for not scalable... themselves are impossible to read like some of these changes are extensive"

### 2. Reality of the problem — the investor third-party-verifies it as "still unsolved"

- The investor themselves stated and agreed: "**There's something there. It's a problem that hasn't been solved yet.**"
- What matters here is that **the very person with money on the line in this space is third-party-verifying the reality of the problem**.

Transcript (verbatim, relevant excerpt):
> "There's something there. And there's something there. It's not a problem that has been fixed yet. I agree."

### 3. Backing for the Business Model — the revenue curve of code review SaaS

The typical ramp-up pattern for code-review-related startups, as described by the investor:

- Early on, the **revenue cycle is "basically dead."** A period with no revenue continues.
- In the first year, even the investors themselves grow anxious, wondering "is this company going to die?"
- **After a year, it survives "magically," as if by magic.**
- Starting the following year, **it moves into normal revenue.**

Transcript (verbatim, relevant excerpt):
> "the revenue cycle for like a code reviews, like, basically dead and you spent about a year wondering... Is this company going to die or not? ... After a year. Something magical happens and that is not dead... The year after that... it sort of goes into like normal revenues."

**Implication (pitch strategy note):** Code review SaaS has a market characteristic of "slow to ramp, but monetizes if it survives." If pursuing commercialization after the hackathon, funding and expectation-setting need to price in this "valley." Conversely, judges (the investor audience) already know this curve, so thin early traction is already priced in — the point to strike home is "why this will survive (founder-market fit, timing)."
